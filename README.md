### Task 1 & Task 2: Gaussian Processes Prior Sampling, Gaussian Process Regression
** Gaussian Processes & GP Regression

Gaussian Processes is a whole vector of Gaussian (Normal) distribution, like $X = (X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n)$. If they're all jointly Gaussian, we write $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$, where $\mu$ is a vector of means and $\Sigma$ is a covariance matrix that says how every pair of variables relates to each other.

A stochastic process takes this one step further: instead of a finite vector, you have an entire family of random variables. A Gaussian Process (GP) is a stochastic process where, no matter which finite handful of points you pick, those points are always jointly Gaussian together.

a GP isn't a distribution over numbers — it's a distribution over functions. Every time we "sample" from a GP, we don't get a single value, we get a whole function. And a GP is completely described by just two things:

1. A mean function $m(t)$ — usually just set to zero, meaning "no built-in trend, we're staying neutral until we see data."
2. A covariance function (kernel) $k(s,t)$ — this tells you how correlated the function's value at point $s$ is with its value at point $t$.

The kernel is really important here. It decides whether the sampled functions come out smooth, or jagged. Two different kernels, same exact sampling process, completely different vibes.

** The RBF Kernel — smooth and dependable

$$k_{RBF}(x,x') = \sigma^2 \exp\left(-\frac{\|x-x'\|^2}{2\ell^2}\right)$$

This kernel only cares about how far apart two points are, not where they sit on the number line. $\sigma^2$ controls the overall amplitude. $\ell$ (the length scale) controls how fast correlation decays with distance.

** The Brownian Motion Kernel — rough and wandering

$$k_{BM}(s,t) = \min(s,t)$$

This one models actual Brownian motion — the classic "random walk"

A few defining quirks:

* It always starts at zero: $W(0) = 0$.
* Its variance at any point $t$ is just $t$ itself — meaning your uncertainty about where the path is keeps growing the further out you go.
* The increments (how much it moves between two time points) are independent of each other.

We created samples for both kernels(with tiny noise) and scetched them.

** GP Regression — turning the prior into a posterior

We start with a GP prior (every smooth function is equally plausible), and then we condition on some observations to figure out which functions are still plausible once that data is taken into account.

The assumption is that our observed data was generated by some unknown function $f$, but corrupted with a bit of random noise:

$$y_{i} = f(x_{i}) + \epsilon_{i}, \quad \epsilon_{i} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{y}^{2})$$

And $f$ itself is assumed to come from a GP prior, $f \sim \mathcal{GP}(0, k)$. We have a training set — some $x$ values where we actually have noisy measurements — and a test set, a bunch of other $x$ values where we'd like predictions.

Since $f$ is a GP and the noise is Gaussian, it turns out our training outputs $y$ and our desired test outputs $f_{*}$ are jointly Gaussian together:

$$\begin{bmatrix} y \\ f_{*} \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \begin{bmatrix} K(X,X)+\sigma_{y}^{2} I_{n} & K(X,X_{*}) \\ K(X_{*},X) & K(X_{*},X_{*}) \end{bmatrix}\right)$$

Each part of this big covariance matrix is just the kernel evaluated between different groups of points: training-vs-training, training-vs-test, and test-vs-test. The $\sigma_{y}^{2} I_{n}$ term tucked into the top-left block is what accounts for the measurement noise sitting on top of the true function.

Linear Gaussian Conditioning Theorem says that if you know two variables are jointly Gaussian, you can derive the exact conditional distribution of one given the other — and it's still Gaussian. Applying that here gives us the posterior predictive distribution:

$$\mu_{*} = K(X_{*},X)\left[K(X,X)+\sigma_{y}^{2} I_{n}\right]^{-1} y$$

$$\Sigma_{*} = K(X_{*},X_{*}) - K(X_{*},X)\left[K(X,X)+\sigma_{y}^{2} I_{n}\right]^{-1} K(X,X_{*})$$

In plain English:

* $\mu_{*}$ is our model's best single guess for the function at each test point
* $\Sigma_{*}$ tells us how confident that guess is. It starts at the prior's level of uncertainty and then subtracts away however much uncertainty gets resolved thanks to nearby data.

So, close to our training points, the model is confident (uncertainty shrinks down close to the noise level). Far away from any training point, the model has nothing to go on, so it falls back to being just as uncertain as it was before seeing any data at all.

We simulated this whole scenario using $\sin(x)$ as the "secret" true function. You only get to see it through 9 noisy measurements taken at $x = -4, -3, \dots, 4$. Meanwhile, we'd like predictions across a much finer grid spanning the full range from -5 to 5.

Once everything's computed, the picture shows: near our 9 observed points, the predicted curve hugs tightly to $\sin(x)$, and the shaded "confidence band" around it stays thin — the model is sure there because it has real evidence. But in the edges (before -4 or after 4), that confidence band visibly widens. And if we lower the noise_var even more (like 1e-8) we can clearly see that the confidence band in the gaps between training points is wider than the confidence band exactly in training points(since the model is now completely sure in training points, the length of confidence interval is basicaly zero).(we aded an extra plot)

### Task 4: Langevin Dynamics (Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm - ULA)
> **🎯 Objective:** Sample from an intractable or complex probability distribution (a bi-modal Gaussian mixture) by simulating a continuous-time Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE).

**Explanation:**
To sample from a complex bi-modal Gaussian mixture, we simulated a continuous-time Stochastic Differential Equation using the Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA). We formulated the Langevin diffusion process and defined an unnormalized bi-modal log-density function with peaks at $-2$ and $2$. Using central finite differences, we implemented a numerical score function $\nabla \log \pi(x)$ to calculate the gradients of the log-density. The simulation was driven by the Euler-Maruyama discrete time-stepping update, carefully tuned with a step size of $\epsilon = 0.02$ to balance mixing speed against discretization error. The final plots of the particle trajectory across the first 500 steps, along with the density histogram of the accumulated samples, confirm that the process successfully transitioned between modes and converged to the exact target stationary distribution.
